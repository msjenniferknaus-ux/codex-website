5. Deployment Guidance
Website application
Use Editorial Gold for major headings and Mustard Sunshine only as a selective accent. Keep Soft Sand and Warm Cream as the dominant background neutrals so pages feel airy and editorial rather than heavy.
Buttons & interface
Buttons should feel soft, refined, and lightly vintage, with warm neutral fills and subtle borders. The interface should support the content, not overpower it.
Photography direction
Use realistic, sunlit, polished imagery: chic streets, cafķs, hotels, city corners, table settings, and elegant travel details. Avoid heavy HDR, neon yellow, or cartoon-like styling.
Contributor rule
When creating new materials, start with the approved fonts and choose from the approved palette only. Introduce mustard and marigold as highlights, not dominant background colors.

7. Content Architecture, Taxonomy & Page Nesting SOP
This section defines the operating rules for slugs, categories, tags, taxonomies, and parent-child page structure so every new section of the site is built consistently and can scale without becoming messy.
Purpose of structure
* Use pages for evergreen destinations, major hubs, and index-style content.
* Use posts for articles, guides, lists, recommendations, seasonal features, and editorial stories.
* Every new page or post must fit into an existing architecture before it is drafted.
Core rule
* Build the site like a publication, not like a pile of individual pages.
* Hierarchy must be visible in navigation, URL structure, internal links, and the WordPress parent-child page settings.
* When in doubt, choose the option that makes related content easier to browse.
Recommended content model
Content typeUse forExamplesParent pagesMajor site hubs that organize child pages and clustersTravel, Food, Self-Care, Home, AboutChild pagesEvergreen sub-hubs nested under parent pagesTravel / City Guides, Travel / Weekend Getaways, Food / City Food GuidesPostsEditorial articles and searchable content piecesA Weekend in Boston, Best Cafķs in Charleston, 10 Chic Hotels in ParisCategoriesPrimary post organization onlyCity Guides, Weekend Getaways, Restaurants, Travel TipsTagsOptional cross-labels for themes, cities, or moodsBoston, East Coast, Girls Trip, Cozy HotelsTaxonomy rules
* Categories are for broad editorial organization and should be selected deliberately. Do not stack too many categories on a single post.
* Tags are secondary descriptors only. Use them to connect related content across categories, cities, trip types, moods, or seasons.
* Pages should not be treated like posts. Evergreen pages should be nested using parent-child page settings, not improvised with tags.
* Keep taxonomy names reader-friendly. Use title case in the dashboard label, and clean lowercase hyphenated slugs in the URL.
* Avoid near-duplicate taxonomy terms. Example: choose either ōCity Guidesö or ōCity Guide,ö not both.
Slug rules
* Use lowercase only.
* Use hyphens instead of spaces or underscores.
* Remove filler words when possible.
* Keep slugs short, readable, and stable once published.
* Do not include dates in evergreen URLs unless the content is truly date-bound.
* Use one canonical naming convention for similar content types.
Slug pattern examples
ContentRecommended slugNotesParent page/travel/Top-level site hubChild page/travel/city-guides/Nested under TravelCity guide page/travel/city-guides/boston/Evergreen destination pageWeekend article post/travel/weekend-getaways/boston-weekend-getaway/Editorial post, not a pageRestaurant article post/food/city-guides/best-restaurants-boston/Food cluster contentSelf-care article post/self-care/routines/sunday-reset-routine/Topic + subtype + article slugParent-child page SOP
1. Create the top-level parent page first. Example: Travel.
2. Create the sub-page and assign its parent in WordPress. Example: City Guides becomes a child of Travel.
3. Keep the child page purpose broad enough to hold multiple related entries.
4. Only create a new child page when it will support a clear cluster of content, not one isolated article.
5. Link parent pages to their child pages and child pages back to parent hubs so users can wander naturally.
6. Before publishing, confirm the page slug, parent assignment, menu placement, breadcrumbs if used, and internal linking are all aligned.
Governance rules for consistency
AreaRuleWhy it mattersCategories per postUsually 1 primary category, occasionally 2Prevents clutter and keeps archives meaningfulTags per postUse only relevant tags, generally 3-8 maximumImproves cross-linking without creating noiseNew taxonomy creationDo not create new categories or tags casuallyAvoids duplication and fragmented archivesPage nestingNest pages only when a real parent-child relationship existsKeeps the site understandable to users and search enginesSlug edits after publishingAvoid changing published slugs unless necessaryProtects SEO and prevents broken linksNaming consistencyMatch terminology across menus, categories, page titles, and slugsCreates a polished editorial systemContent placement decision rule
* If the content is evergreen and acts like a hub, make it a page.
* If the content is an article, list, guide, or feature, make it a post.
* If the content should live under a broader section permanently, assign a parent page.
* If the content only needs topical labeling, use categories and tags rather than page nesting.
Starter taxonomy recommendation for Slap of Sunshine
* Parent pages: Home, Start Here, Travel, Food, Self-Care, Home & Garden, About.
* Travel child pages: City Guides, Weekend Getaways, Travel Tips, Hotels, Things to Do.
* Food child pages or categories: City Food Guides, Restaurants, Cafķs, Brunch, Bars & Drinks.
* Self-Care child pages or categories: Routines, Wellness Escapes, Beauty, Reflection, Reset Guides.
* Tags: city names, region names, trip styles, seasonal themes, mood descriptors, and recurring brand themes.
Editorial rule of thumb
Do not create content first and figure out its place later. Assign every item its structural home before writing, designing, or publishing it.
